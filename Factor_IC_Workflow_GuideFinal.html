What to bring to the session
Be specific about what you want. "NEW concept, GLP-1 lane, Structure B" gets better output than "write me some ads." State the job type, lane, and any constraints upfront.

Name the asset when scaling. "ADAPT #20 into Mediterranean" tells Claude exactly what mechanism to decompose. Vague requests like "something like our skeptic ads" force guesswork.

Bring your hypothesis. "I think there's an angle around GLP-1 muscle loss that we haven't tested" gives Claude a starting point that's grounded in your account knowledge.
Steering phrases that redirect Claude
"Too safe" / "Push harder" — Claude shifts to lower-saturation hook archetypes and cultural moment territory.

"Too generic" — Claude adds specificity: numbers, named situations, viewer behaviors. Mines the CSV for authentic phrasing.

"More like Asset #X" — Claude decomposes X's mechanism and applies it to the current concept.

"Not converting" / "Not DR enough" — Claude tightens the benefit sequence, checks relief-before-features order, adds barrier-busting.

"Feels like an ad" — Claude shifts to first-person voice, removes brand language from the first 10 seconds, uses confessional hooks.
Working with ads outside the library
Claude can't watch video files. If you want to scale, adapt, or analyze an ad that isn't in the 42-asset library, you need to provide one of these:

Option 1 — Scene-by-scene breakdown (best). Use your Gemini video breakdown workflow to generate a structured table (scene #, visual description, on-screen text, VO script). Paste the output directly into the chat. This gives Claude the same level of detail it has for library assets.

Option 2 — VO transcript. Paste the spoken script. Claude can analyze the copy engine, hook mechanism, and persuasion arc from the words alone — but will ask clarifying questions about visuals if needed for Entity ID or format analysis.

Option 3 — Text description. Describe what happens in the ad: who's in it, what they say, what's shown, how long it runs. Less precise but workable for quick analysis.

The more detail you provide, the sharper the decomposition. A full scene-by-scene breakdown gets you the same quality analysis as any library asset. A one-sentence description gets you a surface-level read.
What Claude won't do until you ask
Visual direction — no shot lists or visual suggestions until you say "ready for visuals" or "format for production."

Full script formatting — no timestamps until you say "format this" or "ready to brief."

Strategy essays — Claude opens with hooks or options, not lane analysis. If you want strategic context, ask for it explicitly.
